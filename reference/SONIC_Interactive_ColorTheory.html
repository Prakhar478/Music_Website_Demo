What is the Color Wheel?

The color wheel is the designer's most fundamental tool — a circular map of all colors arranged by their natural relationships. First described by Isaac Newton in 1666 when he split white light through a prism, it reveals how colors are born, how they blend, and how they fight each other. Every intentional design decision in SŌNIC starts here.

The 4 Color Schemes — and Why We Chose Complementary

A color scheme is the systematic rule for how colors relate on the wheel. It's not about picking colors you "like" — it's about picking a relationship that serves the design's emotion and purpose. Below are all four standard schemes. SŌNIC uses Complementary — here's why each option was considered.

Analogous
The Concept

Colors that sit side-by-side on the wheel — they share undertones and feel naturally related. There's no tension, no fight. The eye moves easily across them like it would across a sunset.

Mood & Emotion

Calm · Cohesive · Natural · Soothing. Great for designs that want to feel effortless or serene — wellness apps, nature photography, ambient experiences.

Real world: Instagram's gradient logo — pink flows into orange flows into yellow. All neighbors.
🚫 Not SŌNIC — A music platform needs tension and contrast. Analogous is too calm; it wouldn't make a "Play" button feel urgent.
Adjacent segments · no contrast
Complementary
#0D9488
#F97316
The Concept

Two colors directly opposite on the wheel — 180° apart. They have nothing in common, which is exactly what creates maximum visual tension and vibrancy. When placed side-by-side they make each other appear more intense — this is called simultaneous contrast.

Why It Works for Music

Music is built on contrast — verse vs chorus, build vs drop, loud vs quiet. Teal (cool, stable, trustworthy) vs Orange (warm, exciting, urgent) is that same emotional push-pull expressed in color. The orange Play button on a teal-accented dark stage is impossible to ignore.

Real world: LA Lakers. Pure complementary — purple and gold fight and win together.
✅ SŌNIC's choice. Teal = information, depth, trust. Orange = action, energy, play. The dark background acts as a concert stage — both colors glow harder in the dark.
Opposite segments · 180° · max contrast
Triadic
Red
Yellow
Blue
The Concept

Three colors placed 120° apart — evenly triangulated around the wheel. Each color is different enough to stand out, but the triangular structure creates an underlying balance. It's the most colorful of all schemes.

Mood & Emotion

Vibrant · Playful · Energetic · Colorful. Works best when one color dominates and the other two serve as accents — otherwise it can feel chaotic.

Real world: Classic primary color toys & children's media — inherently triadic.
🚫 Not SŌNIC — Three strong hues make brand identity complex. A music app needs one dominant color for CTAs, not three competing for attention.
Triangle · 3 equal points · 120° apart
Monochromatic
The Concept

One single hue in multiple shades (darker), tints (lighter with white), and tones (muted with grey). "Mono" = one, "chroma" = color. It's the most disciplined of all schemes — every color is literally the same color, just in different lighting.

Mood & Emotion

Elegant · Minimal · Sophisticated · Premium. Zero color clashing is possible. The weakness is it can feel flat — there's no way to use color to guide the eye toward a CTA.

Real world: Tiffany & Co. — one signature aqua used across every single touchpoint.
🚫 Not SŌNIC — Beautiful but impractical for interaction design. With one hue, an orange "Play" button is impossible — and that button is everything.
One hue · shades & tints only

Why These Specific Colors?

Picking a scheme is step one. Picking the exact colors within that scheme is step two — and it requires understanding color psychology. Orange = action, warmth, energy (every button, every play trigger). Teal = trust, technology, depth (every badge, border, and progress bar). The near-black background behaves like a concert stage — spotlights always look brighter in the dark.

Why Complementary Works for a Music Platform
🎵
Energy = Contrast
Music is built on tension — drop, build, release. Teal (calm) vs Orange (energetic) is that same push-pull expressed visually. The colors feel like sound feels.
🎯
Orange = Action
Every interactive element that asks you to do something — play, start, get started — is orange. Orange is warm, forward-moving, impossible to ignore.
🌊
Teal = Depth & Trust
Teal carries the calm of blue (reliable, digital, trustworthy) with a hint of green (fresh, alive). It tells you: this is information, this is structure, this is safe.
🎭
The Stage Effect
Deep black background = concert lighting. Any color looks more vivid against near-black than against white or grey. The darkness amplifies both teal and orange simultaneously.
⚡
Simultaneous Contrast
A law of optics: complementary colors placed side-by-side make each other appear more saturated than they actually are. Teal makes orange look more orange. Orange makes teal look more teal.
🧠
Brand Distinctiveness
Spotify uses green. Apple Music uses red. Most music apps live in the warm-dark space. Teal-and-orange is uncommon enough to be memorable — users will recall SŌNIC by its colors alone.

How Screens Make Color — The RGB Model

Every pixel on your screen is a microscopic light source emitting exactly three colors: Red, Green, and Blue. By mixing these three light beams at different intensities (0–255 each), screens can reproduce 16.7 million unique colors. This is called additive color — because you add light, not subtract it. More light = brighter. All three at 255 = pure white. All three at 0 = pure black. This is fundamentally different from mixing paint.
